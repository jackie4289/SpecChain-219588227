Application: [Medito]
This project aims to identify the differences between manual, automated, and hybrid software requirement workflows. The manual pipeline provided me with the most control, as I had to manually read and group the Medito reviews before turning them into personas, requirements, and tests. This made the artifacts more realistic, but it is very labour-intensive and only allowed me to cover a small part of the cleaned dataset. This is clearly shown in the metrics. The manual pipeline only covers 0.0187 of the 4006 cleaned reviews, but I was still able to produce complete downstream artifacts with 5 personas, 10 requirements, and 20 tests.
The automated pipeline is the strongest in scale and consistency. It cleanly grouped all the datasets, which means the automated pipeline covered 1.0 of the cleaned reviews, and its traceability links were much higher than the other pipelines. Every automated requirement and test is linked through personas and IDs, so the pipeline is structurally strong. However, the automated outputs are not always strong systematically. Some of the personas' names are generic and generated requirements that were broader. For example, the automated specification required sleep content to continue for at least 8 hours and required at least 80% of sleep stories to be 30 minutes or longer. The outputs were coherent, but felt like the model extrapolated rather than provided careful summaries of the reviews.
The hybrid pipeline produced the best final artifacts overall. Starting from automated outputs saved time, but the revision step made the results more believable and testable. In the hybrid specification, vague or inflated claims were replaced with narrower behaviours that could be traced directly to review themes, such as optional donation prompts, separate sleep sections, repeat and queue support, named emotional-support categories, favourites from the home screen, and sync of streaks and lessons across devices within 60 seconds. Those requirements were easier to verify than many of the automated ones. I think the hybrid personas were the clearest because they preserved the broad themes that were found automatically, but removed unsupported assumptions and tightened the goals, pain points, and constraints. 
The most useful lesson from the project was that strong traceability numbers do not mean strong requirements quality. The automated pipeline has the best coverage and the highest number of traceability links, but still needs human reviews to make the outputs more precise and evidence-based. The manual pipeline is more trustworthy in tone but costly in time. This is why the hybrid pipeline gave the best balance between speed and quality. If I have to choose one pipeline to continue with a real project, I would choose the hybrid approach because it keeps the efficiency of automation but leaves room for requirements judgment, which turned out to be the difference between plausible output and reliable output.
