Application: [Medito]
This project made the differences between manual, automated, and hybrid requirements workflows very visible. The manual pipeline gave me the most control over interpretation because I read and grouped the Medito reviews myself before turning them into personas, requirements, and tests. That made the manual artifacts feel more grounded, but it was also the slowest pipeline and it only covered a small portion of the cleaned dataset. The metrics show that clearly: the manual pipeline covered only 0.0187 of the 4006 cleaned reviews, even though it still produced complete downstream artifacts with 5 personas, 10 requirements, and 20 tests. 
The automated pipeline was strongest in scale and consistency. It grouped the full cleaned dataset, which is why its review coverage reached 1.0 and its traceability links were much higher than the other pipelines. Every automated requirement and test was still linked through personas and IDs, so the pipeline looked strong structurally. However, the automated outputs were not always strong semantically. Some persona names became generic, such as "Mindful Seeker" and "Anxious Individual," and some generated requirements were broader or stronger than the evidence justified. For example, the automated specification required sleep content to continue for at least 8 hours and required at least 80% of sleep stories to be 30 minutes or longer. Those outputs were coherent, but they felt like model extrapolations rather than careful summaries of the reviews. 
The hybrid pipeline produced the best final artifacts overall. Starting from the automated outputs saved time, but the revision step made the results much more believable and testable. In the hybrid specification, vague or inflated claims were replaced with narrower behaviors that could be traced directly to review themes, such as optional donation prompts, separate sleep sections, repeat and queue support, named emotional-support categories, favorites from the home screen, and sync of streaks and lessons across devices within 60 seconds. Those requirements were easier to defend and easier to verify than many of the automated ones. I also think the hybrid personas were the clearest because they preserved the broad themes found automatically but removed unsupported assumptions and tightened the goals, pain points, and constraints. 
The most useful lesson from the project was that strong traceability numbers do not automatically mean strong requirements quality. The automated pipeline had the best coverage and the highest number of traceability links, but it still needed human review to make the outputs precise and evidence-based. The manual pipeline was more trustworthy in tone, while the hybrid pipeline gave the best balance between speed and quality. If I had to choose one pipeline to continue with in a real project, I would choose the hybrid approach because it keeps the efficiency of automation but still leaves room for requirements judgment, which turned out to be the difference between plausible output and reliable output.
